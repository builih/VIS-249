Glass as a Machine
for Light

How glass bends, stores, filters, and performs light across science and art

Glass is often described as transparent, but transparency is not the absence of behavior. Throughout this course, I began to understand glass as an active optical material that bends, reflects, absorbs, and scatters light in highly controlled ways.

Surface & Light

02 — Reflection vs Transmission

One of the first concepts that changed how I looked at glass was the STAR model: scattered, transmitted, absorbed, and reflected light together account for nearly all optical behavior. Even "clear" glass reflects part of the environment while transmitting the rest. Looking through glass always means looking at two images simultaneously: the world beyond the surface and the reflected world in front of it.

03 — Matte vs Polished Surface

Coldworking made optical physics tactile. A rough matte surface scatters incoming light diffusely, causing glass to appear translucent or frosted. After polishing, the same material suddenly becomes transparent because the smoother surface reduces scattering and allows more coherent transmission. The difference visually appears dramatic, but physically it comes from microscopic changes in surface roughness.

04 — Grit Progression

Each grit stage progressively removed larger scratches left by the previous abrasive. At coarse grits, the glass surface strongly scattered light, preventing clear image formation. As the scratches became smaller than visible wavelengths, the surface transitioned from opaque to transparent. Polishing therefore became less about "making something shiny" and more about engineering the path of light itself.

05 — Microscope Surface

Under magnification, polished and roughened glass surfaces reveal entirely different optical landscapes. Deep scratches and irregularities act as scattering centers that redirect light into many directions. Observing the surface microscopically helped connect tactile processes directly to optical behavior.

Refraction & Internal Light

06 — Thick Cast Glass

Large cast glass sculptures behave almost like solidified lenses. Because glass has a higher refractive index than air, light bends as it enters and exits the material. Thick forms can trap, redirect, and internally reflect light, producing glowing regions that appear illuminated from within. The object becomes less like a solid sculpture and more like a volume of controlled optical pathways.

07 — Light Path Diagram

Curved surfaces redirect light according to Snell's Law. In rounded glass forms, light entering from the sides can refract toward the center, creating brighter internal regions and darker edges. Artists such as Libenský and Brychtová intentionally used geometry to manipulate these effects, designing forms that shape light as much as mass.

08 — Internal Reflection

When light traveling inside glass strikes the surface above the critical angle, it reflects internally instead of escaping. This phenomenon, called total internal reflection, allows glass to guide light through its interior. The same principle enables fiber optic communication systems, but in sculptural glass it creates glowing edges and luminous interiors.

09 — Fiber Optics / Corning

The Corning Museum made clear that the same material principles appear across both art and technology. Fiber optics use extremely pure glass to transmit information through controlled internal reflections. Scientific precision and aesthetic experience become closely related: both depend on manipulating how light moves through transparent matter.

Color as Optical Behavior

Image 10 Colored glass appearing darker in thicker regions

10 — Color in Thickness

Color in glass is volumetric rather than purely surface-based. Thicker regions absorb more wavelengths because light travels farther through the material. As a result, the same glass composition can appear pale at thin edges and deeply saturated at thicker sections. Thickness therefore becomes a way of sculpting color itself.

11 — Shift / Dichroic Glass

Certain glasses change color depending on the illumination source. Rare earth ions such as neodymium contain narrow absorption bands that interact differently with fluorescent and incandescent light spectra. This means color is not fixed inside the object but emerges dynamically from the interaction between material and environment.

Image 12 Lycurgus Cup in reflected vs transmitted light
Replace with your photo
12 — The Lycurgus Cup

The Lycurgus Cup demonstrates how metallic nanoparticles can radically alter optical behavior. In reflected light the cup appears green, while in transmitted light it glows red. Nanoparticles scatter and absorb different wavelengths depending on lighting geometry, producing one of history's earliest examples of nanophotonic color engineering.

13 — Tiffany Favrile / Iridescent Glass

Iridescent glass creates color not through pigments alone, but through thin-film interference. Light reflects from multiple interfaces within microscopic surface coatings, and certain wavelengths constructively interfere while others cancel out. As the viewing angle changes, the visible colors shift continuously across the surface.

Stress, Fragility, Memory

Image 14 Comparing fracture patterns in tempered vs annealed glass
Replace with your photo
14 — Tempered vs Annealed Fracture

Glass failure patterns reveal hidden internal stress states. Annealed glass fractures into large, dangerous shards because cracks propagate freely. Tempered glass contains compressive surface stress that interrupts crack propagation, causing it to fragment into smaller pieces. Stress therefore becomes visible only at the moment of failure.

15 — Polarized Stress

Under polarized light, internal stress becomes visible as bands of color. These stress patterns form because strained regions alter how light travels through the glass. What normally appears optically invisible suddenly emerges as a complex internal map of thermal history and mechanical forces.

16 — Kiln Schedule / Annealing

Annealing is fundamentally about controlling how quickly thermal energy leaves the material. If the exterior cools too rapidly compared to the interior, stress accumulates as different regions contract unevenly. The kiln schedule therefore functions as both a scientific protocol and a choreography of molecular relaxation.

Section 05

Process & Transformation

17 — Wax Model / Mold

Kilncasting transforms materials through multiple states: wax becomes void, molten glass becomes solid, and temporary molds disappear after firing. Each stage leaves traces in the final object. The process revealed how glassmaking is less about imposing form onto matter and more about negotiating with thermal flow, viscosity, gravity, and time.

18 — Molten Glass / Furnace

At high temperatures, glass exists in a state between liquid and solid. Its viscosity changes continuously rather than abruptly, allowing it to slowly deform, flow, and settle into molds. Observing molten glass challenged my assumptions about materials existing in fixed states.

19 — My Own Work

My own interaction with glass shifted from seeing it as a passive material to understanding it as an optical and thermal system. Surface finish, thickness, cooling history, and lighting conditions all became embedded into the final appearance of the work. Material process and scientific behavior could no longer be separated.

Conclusion

By the end of the semester, I no longer experienced glass simply as transparent. I began to see it as a material that stores heat histories, redirects light, reveals stress, and transforms depending on angle, thickness, and illumination. The science of glass did not reduce its mystery; instead, understanding the physics made its visual behavior feel even more complex and alive.
